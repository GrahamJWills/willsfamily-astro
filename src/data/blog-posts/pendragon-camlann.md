And so it befell in the five hundred and sixty-fourth year that King Arthur and Sir Mordred came with their hosts unto the plain of Camlann, and there was the last great battle fought that ever was fought in the realm of Logres, and God in his mercy grant it be the last --- such a battle as was never seen in Britain before nor since, for both sides were full of great knights, and they fought with such ferocity that the ground could not drink the blood fast enough, and the field turned to a red mud beneath their feet.

Among the first of the great knights to fall was Sir Griflet, who had served the court since the early days of the king's glory, and he died as he had lived, with a lance in his hand. And Sir Tor fell also before the morning was much advanced, and he made a good end. And Sir Dinadin, who had drawn the sword upon the adder and thus begun all the slaughter, was himself slain before the first hour was out, and many said there was a bitter justice in it.

Now of the four great player knights who had ridden together for five and thirty years, this is the account of how each met their end at Camlann.

Sir Osgar, son of Sir Owain, knight of the Round Table these many years, met his death where a stream crossed the field. He was pressed hard and took a grievous blow upon his helm that set the world reeling, and his feet slipped in the bed of the stream, and the current took him, and he went under, and did not rise again. And men said afterward that the water ran red for a year from that stream.

But it is also told that Sir Cynric of Escavalon --- who had retired from the adventurous life these many years, being no longer a man of war but only a good man who loved his husband --- saw Sir Osgar go down, and ran to his aid, and the men of Mordred's host cut him down before he could reach the water. And some who were there said it was the saddest thing they saw all day.

Sir Elias, son of Sir Hywel, the savior of Camelot, who had spent his life belonging to no one and finding his own path, was killed by a giant among Mordred's host --- a creature from the far north that swung a tree-trunk as a man might swing a broom. The blow struck him full upon the chest and caved in the plate of his armor and Sir Elias's ribs broke inward, and he fell and did not rise.

Dame Gweneth, daughter of Sir Cian, who had achieved the Holy Grail, fought all that long day and into the evening, and did not fall easily. As the light failed, she and Sir Damian found each other in the press and fought together, back to back, and between them they brought down two of Mordred's finest knights. But the effort of it was the last that remained in them, and they fell together, as they had ridden together, and Gweneth did not rise again. And they buried her later with her husband, Rhainallt, who had died close by. And it is said that ever after their grave was always surrounded by a pleasant smell, as if of roses and honey.

Sir Damian, son of Sir Duncan, the Knight of Love and the husband of Ahvielle came back to himself slowly, as a man who has been knocked from his horse comes back, not knowing at first where he is or which way is up. He found the king.

Arthur was pale and fading and entirely himself, and he gave Sir Damian Excalibur and sent him to the lake with the sword. And Damian threw it, as he was bid, and a hand in white samite rose from the water and caught it by the hilt, and the hand waved it thrice in a circle, and drew it under. And the mist that had been gathering at the lake's edge all afternoon rolled in faster than mist has any right to move, and out of it came a boat. Morgan LeFay stood in the prow, and two women were with her whom Damian did not recognize, in grey and silver, and they said nothing.

The women took Arthur aboard, and the body of Sir Osgar was brought to the boat by Morgan's ladies. And as they laid him in the boat, one of the grey-clad women knelt beside him and placed her hands upon his chest, and spoke low words that caused the water to run out of Sir Osgar's lungs and the color to come back into his face. He drew a long slow breath as a man draws breath after a deep sleep, and opened his eyes. He did not speak. He looked at the sky above the mist, and then at Damian, and raised himself to sit at the side of the boat.

And Damian looked at Morgan, and Morgan looked at him, and she held out her hand, and he took it, and stepped into the boat. As the boat moved away and the shapes aboard were nearly lost to the fog --- Arthur sat up and embraced the two knights  and the mist closed over the water entirely, and the lake was empty, and there was no one left to see it.

They lie now in the tomb below Avalon, all three of them, awaiting the hour that Britain shall need them again. For men say in many parts of England that King Arthur is not dead, but was carried by the will of Our Lord into another place; and men say that he shall come again. I will not say that it shall be so, but rather I will say: here in this world he changed his life. Yet many men say that there is written upon his tomb this verse: 

**Hic Iacet Arthurus, Rex Quondam, Rexque Futurus**.

And thus Sir Bedivere, with the hermit that had once been Archbishop of Canterbury, lived at this site in prayer and fasting and in holy works; and thither came Sir Bedivere and put upon himself poor clothes, and served the hermit full lowly in fasting and in prayers. And thus they lived in their prayers and fastings and great abstinence. And when Queen Guenever understood that King Arthur was slain and all the noble knights, then she stole away with five ladies with her, and so she went to Amesbury, and there she let make herself a nun, and wore white clothes and black, and great penance she took. If she ever met her erstwhile lover, Sir Hywel, again, this story does not speak of it.
